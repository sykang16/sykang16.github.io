Introduction

The Inventory of Owned and Leased Properties (IOLP) dataset contains information about properties owned or leased by the General Services Administration (GSA). These properties are located across the United States, Puerto Rico, Guam, and American Samoa. The dataset helps people understand how government buildings and spaces are used, whether they are owned or rented, and provides details about their location, size, and condition.

Interactive Map

Interactive Map of GSA-Owned and Leased Properties

The interactive map visualizes GSA-owned and leased properties across the United States. Each point represents a property, and the colors distinguish properties by state. You can hover over points to see detailed information about each property, including: The property's name, The city and state, The building's rentable square feet, The available square feet, The lease expiration date

Key insights include the widespread distribution of properties across all states, with clusters in major cities and states with higher activity. This map allows users to explore the details of each property and understand its geographic distribution interactively.

Heatmap

Heatmap of Property Locations by Rentable Square Feet

Generated a heatmap to visualize the distribution of property locations based on the Building Rentable Square Feet feature.
● The heatmap reveals a concentration of properties with large rentable square footage in certain regions of the USA. The most prominent clusters appear in areas like Texas, California, and parts of the Northeast.
● The color scale, ranging from purple (low values) to yellow (high values), indicates areas with smaller or larger rentable square feet, helping us easily identify high-density regions of large properties.

This visualization allows us to make informed decisions about property distribution and better understand geographical trends in building sizes.

Federal

Federal Properties in California

● Coastal Concentration: A significant number of federal properties are located along the California coast, particularly in major metropolitan areas like San Francisco, Los Angeles, and San Diego. This concentration likely reflects the importance of these areas for trade, transportation, and military operations.
● Inland Presence: While the coast dominates, there is a notable presence of federal properties inland, including in cities like Sacramento, Fresno, and Bakersfield. This indicates that federal activities extend beyond coastal regions and into the state's interior.

Space

Rentable vs. Available Space

General Observations:
● Bubble Size: The size of each bubble represents the rentable square footage of a building. Larger bubbles indicate larger buildings.
● Color Gradient: The color of each bubble corresponds to the utilization rate, with darker colors representing higher utilization rates.

Specific Insights:
● Most Buildings Have Low Utilization Rates: A majority of the buildings have smaller bubbles and darker colors, suggesting they have lower utilization rates.
● Large Buildings Have Higher Utilization Rates: The few larger bubbles are generally lighter in color, indicating that larger buildings tend to have higher utilization rates.
● Wide Range of Rentable Space: The graph shows a wide range of building sizes, from very small to very large.
● Clustering: There are clusters of buildings with similar sizes and utilization rates, suggesting potential trends or patterns in the data.

Potential Implications:
● Efficiency: The low utilization rates for many buildings suggest potential inefficiencies in space utilization.
● Market Demand: The distribution of building sizes and utilization rates might reflect the demand for different types of commercial space in the market.
● Strategic Planning: Understanding the relationship between building size and utilization rate can inform strategic decisions about property management, leasing, and development.

Top States

Top 10 States with the Most Properties

Overall Trend:
● Uneven Distribution: There's a significant difference between the top states and the rest. The top 3 states (CA, TX, and FL) have a significantly higher number of properties compared to the others.

Specific Observations:
● California Dominance: California has the highest number of properties, indicating a strong real estate market.
● Texas and Florida: These states follow closely behind California, suggesting robust real estate sectors in these regions.
● Mid-Atlantic States: States like Virginia, New York, Pennsylvania, and Maryland have a notable presence, indicating a significant real estate market in this region.
● Diverse Geographic Spread: The top 10 states are spread across different regions of the United States, suggesting a nationwide distribution of real estate holdings.

Potential Implications:
● Investment Opportunities: States with a high number of properties might offer significant investment opportunities, particularly in areas with strong economic growth and population growth.
● Market Dynamics: Understanding the distribution of properties across states can help identify regional market trends, such as demand for specific property types or rental rates.
● Risk Management: A diversified portfolio across different states can help mitigate risk associated with economic fluctuations in specific regions

Properties and Population Estimates

For this we have used the population dataset ,the aim is to integrate property data with population estimates for cities in the USA to visualize the relationship between property counts and population using an interactive geographic map.

Insights:
● City-Level Property and Population Trends:
○ The map provides an interactive way to observe cities with high property counts in relation to their population size.
○ Cities with larger populations may not necessarily have proportionally higher property counts, suggesting differences in urban density and development.
● Geographic Distribution:
○ The visualization highlights spatial clustering of cities with significant property counts or population sizes.
○ For example, coastal cities may exhibit higher activity compared to rural or inland regions.
● Potential Patterns:
○ Analyze whether population density correlates with the number of properties in urban areas.
○ Identify outliers where property counts are unusually high or low compared to the population.
